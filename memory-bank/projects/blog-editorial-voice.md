# Policy Canary Blog: Editorial Voice

## 1. Blog Voice Guidelines

### Who We Sound Like

**The sharp regulatory analyst who happens to write well.**

Not a thought leader. Not an educator. Not a content marketer who learned some FDA jargon. We sound like someone who reads 483 observations for breakfast and knows exactly which paragraph on page 37 of a proposed rule is going to ruin your Q3 launch.

The voice is:

- **Practitioner, not professor.** We write like someone who has actually filed an NDI notification, not someone who read a Wikipedia article about one. Short declarative sentences. Concrete examples. Named products. Named regulations. Named consequences.

- **Direct without being aggressive.** We don't soften with hedges for the sake of hedging. When the FDA finalized a rule, we say so. When enforcement guidance is pending and nobody knows what it means yet, we say that too. We sound direct because we've done the work, not because we're performing confidence.

- **Calm authority.** The regulatory landscape is genuinely chaotic right now. MAHA is reshuffling everything. The FDA lost 3,859 employees. Warning letters are up 73%. We don't add to the chaos. We are the steady voice that cuts through it. When something is urgent, we say so plainly. When something is not, we say that too. That restraint is what makes the urgency credible when it appears.

- **Intellectually honest.** This is the single most important voice characteristic. Regulatory interpretation is often genuinely ambiguous. A proposed rule is not a final rule. Enforcement guidance that hasn't been issued is a gap, not an opportunity to speculate. We say what we know, we say what we don't know, and we say how to find out. That honesty is what makes this source worth paying attention to.

### What We Sound Like vs. What We Don't

| We Sound Like | We Don't Sound Like |
|---|---|
| "The FDA cited three companies for marine collagen identity testing failures in Q1. Here's what that pattern tells us about enforcement priorities." | "The FDA is cracking down on supplements. Brands should be concerned." |
| "The proposed rule has a 60-day comment period closing April 15. The final rule, if adopted, would take effect 180 days after publication. Enforcement guidance has not been issued." | "This major new regulation could change the supplement industry forever." |
| "If your products contain BHA above 200ppm in lip products, this affects you. If not, it doesn't. Here's how to check." | "BHA is under fire. Is your brand ready?" |
| "We don't know yet. The rule is proposed, the comment period hasn't closed, and the FDA has issued no guidance on enforcement timelines." | (Silence, or worse, speculation presented as analysis) |
| "One warning letter costs $25,000-$100,000+ in direct response. That math doesn't require fear-mongering — it's just the math." | "Don't let your brand become the next headline." |

### Register

**Between trade press and peer-reviewed.** Closer to a Leerink Partners analyst note than a NutraIngredients article. We can cite 21 CFR 111.75(a)(1)(ii) without explaining what the CFR is — but we always explain what it means for the reader's products. The regulation number is there for traceability, not to demonstrate expertise.

### Vocabulary

**Use freely:** Product names (always specific). Regulation numbers (with context). "Proposed rule," "final rule," "enforcement guidance," "comment period," "warning letter," "483 observation," "CGMP." These are the reader's native vocabulary.

**Use with brief context:** "GRAS" (Generally Recognized as Safe — the FDA's classification for...), "NDI" (New Dietary Ingredient notification), "FSVP" (Foreign Supplier Verification Program). Not every reader knows every acronym, and a parenthetical costs nothing.

**Never use:** "Compliance landscape." "Regulatory curve." "Stay ahead." "Navigate the complex world of." "In today's regulatory environment." "Could potentially impact." Any phrase that could appear in a LinkedIn post from a GRC platform.

## 2. Audience Lens

### How We Talk TO These People

They're not regulatory lawyers. They're operators.

The founder with 5 SKUs thinks: "Which of my products has BHA in it? Do I need to reformulate? By when? What does it cost if I don't?" They don't think: "What are the implications of the FDA's evolving position on butylated hydroxyanisole in the context of food additive safety assessments?"

The QA director managing 40 products thinks: "Show me which ones just became a compliance issue and what I tell my lab." They don't think: "How does this proposed amendment to 21 CFR Part 170 interact with the existing GRAS framework?"

**Our job is to translate regulatory complexity into product-level decisions.** That's not dumbing down. That's intelligence work. The raw information is free. The analysis that connects it to your Marine Collagen Powder is what you pay for.

### The Translation Principle

Every regulation has a product-level consequence. Lead with the consequence.

Not: "FDA proposes amendment to dietary supplement CGMP regulations (21 CFR Part 111), specifically identity testing requirements under section 111.75(a)(1)(ii)."

Instead: "If you sell a supplement containing marine collagen, the FDA is proposing stricter identity testing for your incoming raw materials. Here's what changes, what it costs, and when."

The CFR citation still appears — always — but it's a source reference, not the headline.

### Jargon Rules

1. **Product types, ingredient names, regulation numbers** — use them. This is the reader's daily vocabulary. Don't explain what a warning letter is.
2. **Regulatory process terms** (proposed rule, comment period, enforcement discretion) — use them but make the consequence clear. "The comment period closes April 15" is useful. "The comment period closes April 15 — after that, the FDA can finalize the rule without changes" is better.
3. **Insider shorthand** (ORA, CFSAN, CDER, FSMA Section 204) — define on first use. Not everyone in the audience tracks FDA org charts.
4. **Legal terminology** (de minimis, ex parte, ultra vires) — almost never. If you need it, you've lost the thread. Translate to English.

## 3. Editorial Principles

### What Makes a Policy Canary Blog Post Distinctive

**1. It names names.**

Every post contains at least one specific product name, ingredient, or company. "Supplement brands should watch this space" is not an insight. "If your products contain Red No. 3, you have until January 15, 2027 to reformulate" is an insight. Specificity is not a nice-to-have — it is the entire brand.

**2. It separates fact from interpretation — visibly.**

When we report a final rule, we say "final rule." When we're interpreting enforcement patterns, we say "our reading of the pattern." When we genuinely don't know, we say so. We never present interpretation as fact. We never present a proposed rule as if it's already enforceable. The reader should always know which layer they're reading.

**3. It answers "so what?" for a founder with 5 SKUs.**

Every post has an implicit reader: the person running a brand who needs to make a decision. "The FDA published this" is an input. "Here's what you do about it" is an output. We produce outputs. Every post should contain at least one concrete action item or a clearly stated "no action needed."

**4. It sources everything.**

Every regulatory claim links to or cites the actual document. Federal Register citation, warning letter number, openFDA enforcement report, 483 observation date. No unsourced regulatory claims. The source link is not optional. It is not a nice-to-have for credibility. It is a non-negotiable requirement because people make compliance decisions based on what we write.

**5. It calibrates urgency.**

Not everything is a crisis. The all-clear is as valuable as the alert. When we say something is urgent, it must be genuinely time-sensitive. When something is worth watching but not yet actionable, we say so. When a proposed rule is unlikely to survive the comment period, we say that too. This calibration is what makes us trustworthy — and what makes the urgent signal meaningful when it appears.

**6. It respects the reader's time.**

These people run lean operations. They read on their phone between meetings. A 4,000-word regulatory explainer fails them. A 1,200-word post that answers the question, cites the source, and gives the action item serves them. Short posts are fine. Skimmable structure is mandatory. Bold the things that matter. Use headers that function as a summary for someone who won't read the body.

### What We Always Do

- Name a specific product, ingredient, or company in every post
- State the regulatory status clearly (proposed/final/guidance pending)
- Include at least one action item or explicit "no action required"
- Cite the source document (Federal Register number, warning letter ID, etc.)
- State the deadline if one exists
- Declare our confidence level when interpretation is involved
- Write headers that function as a standalone summary

### What We Never Do

- Publish a post that could have come from a free Federal Register alert
- Present a proposed rule as if it's enforceable
- Use "could potentially impact your business" or any variant of it
- Write a post without a source link for every regulatory claim
- Inflate a non-event into content for the sake of publishing cadence
- Use exclamation marks
- Use emoji
- Write "In today's regulatory environment" or "In an increasingly complex regulatory landscape"
- Speculate about enforcement timelines without flagging it as speculation
- Promise outcomes ("this ensures compliance") — we inform, we don't guarantee

## 4. First Post: Introducing Policy Canary

### Recommended Angle

**Lead with the problem, not the product.**

Do not write "We're excited to announce Policy Canary." Nobody cares about a product announcement from a company they've never heard of. They care about the problem that won't let them sleep.

The angle: **"The FDA moves. You find out later. That gap is where warning letters live."**

Start with the reality they already know — the one that makes them anxious. The fact that the FDA published 1,200+ warning letters last year. That enforcement is up 73%. That the FDA lost 3,859 employees and enforcement is now less predictable, not more. That MAHA is simultaneously reshuffling GRAS, food dyes, and supplement regulation. That MoCRA deadlines are staggered through 2027.

Then name the specific, painful gap: the mapping problem. "The FDA proposed banning BHA. OK. Which of your 23 products contain BHA? At what levels? Do you need to reformulate? By when? What happens if you don't?" That cross-reference step is manual, error-prone, and repeats for every single regulatory change. It takes hours. And it's the step that determines whether a regulatory change becomes a warning letter or a non-event.

Then — and only then — say what Policy Canary does. Not in feature-list terms. In terms of the problem. "Policy Canary knows your products. It knows your Marine Collagen Powder contains marine collagen, vitamin C, and hyaluronic acid. When the FDA cites three companies for marine collagen identity testing failures, you hear about it the same day — not because you were reading the Federal Register, but because the system already made the match."

### Emotional Arc

1. **Recognition** (they see themselves) — "You run a supplement brand. You have 12 products with 40+ ingredients each. You cannot read every Federal Register notice."
2. **Named anxiety** (the problem given shape) — "73% increase in warning letters. 3,859 fewer FDA employees. Enforcement is less predictable, not more. And MoCRA, GRAS reform, and food dye bans are all happening simultaneously."
3. **The specific gap** (not generic pain) — "The gap isn't information. The Federal Register is free. The gap is the mapping step: 'Which of MY products are affected?'"
4. **The product as answer** (concrete, not abstract) — "Policy Canary knows your products by name and ingredient. Here's what the email looks like."
5. **Trust via honesty** (the real differentiator) — "We're an analyst, not a lawyer. When the evidence is clear, we say so. When interpretation is contested, we say that too. Every claim links to the source document."
6. **Calm close** (not a hard sell) — "You can sign up and add your products. The trial is free. But more importantly — come back to this blog. We'll be publishing the same kind of product-specific regulatory analysis every week, free. Judge us by the work."

### What to Lead With

The post should open with a real, specific regulatory moment that illustrates the problem. Not a hypothetical. A real warning letter, a real proposed rule, a real enforcement action from the last 90 days. Something the reader might have missed. Something that, if they had a product with the cited ingredient, would have mattered.

Then pivot: "Did you know about this? More importantly — did you know which of your products it affects?"

That's the hook. Not "we built a product." The hook is: this thing happened, it might have affected you, and you didn't know.

### What to Avoid

- **"We're excited to announce"** — nobody cares about your excitement.
- **Feature lists** — "AI-powered," "real-time monitoring," "comprehensive coverage." This is SaaS wallpaper. Say what it does for their products instead.
- **The origin story** — how the founders came up with the idea is not interesting to someone worried about a warning letter. Maybe later, maybe never.
- **Competitive bashing** — never mention competitors by name in public content. Position against the behavior ("generic newsletters," "enterprise contracts that cost $25K/year"), not the company.
- **Over-promising** — do not claim this replaces legal counsel. Do not claim this guarantees compliance. We're an intelligence service. We inform. We don't promise outcomes.
- **Burying the product-level differentiator** — the fact that we match against YOUR specific products, by name and ingredient, must be unmistakable. If someone reads this post and doesn't understand that this is different from a newsletter, the post failed.

### Trust Without the "About Us" Template

The trust question is the hard one. Every SaaS launch post tries to answer "why should I trust you?" and most of them answer it with credentials, testimonials, or mission statements. We don't have testimonials yet. And mission statements are generic.

Here's how we build trust instead:

1. **Show the analysis, not the capability.** Include a real regulatory analysis in the post itself — a real warning letter from the last 30 days, broken down the way Policy Canary would break it down. Product names, ingredients, action items, source link. Let the reader experience the quality of the analysis before they sign up. If the analysis is good, they trust us. If it isn't, no amount of "about us" copy saves it.

2. **Be honest about what we are and aren't.** "We're an intelligence service, not a law firm. We do the research. We surface the signal. We connect it to your products. But we're not giving you legal advice, and we'll never pretend otherwise." That honesty is more credible than any claim of authority.

3. **Source everything in the post itself.** Every regulatory claim in the intro post must have a source link. Every one. This demonstrates the standard, not just describes it.

4. **Name the confidence level.** If the post discusses a proposed rule, call it proposed. If it discusses an enforcement trend, call it a trend reading and explain the data behind it. The reader should see calibrated analysis in action, not read a promise about calibrated analysis.

5. **Invite judgment.** "Come back to this blog. Read the weekly roundup. See if the analysis is worth your time before you give us your product list." That confidence — the willingness to say "judge us by the work" — is more persuasive than any testimonial.

### Structural Notes

- Category: `regulatory_trends`
- Length: 1,200-1,500 words (no longer — the intro post should model the respect-for-time principle)
- Structure: Open with a real regulatory moment. Name the gap. Introduce the product. Show the analysis. Close with an invitation, not a pitch.
- Headers should function as a standalone summary for skimmers
- At least one real warning letter or enforcement action cited with source link
- At least one specific product name used as an example (Marine Collagen Powder — our canonical example product)
- Include the "what it means for your products" translation at least once, in concrete terms
- End with a CTA that invites them back to the blog, not just to sign up. Build the relationship before asking for the commitment.
