# Blog Post 01 — Research Brief: "Why We Exist"

---

## 1. HOW THE BEST B2B COMPANIES WRITE THEIR "WHY WE EXIST" POST

### The pattern that works

The best founding/introduction posts from B2B SaaS companies share five structural moves:

**Move 1: Anchor to a specific, dated moment of pain — not a generic problem.**
Vanta was founded "in the wake of several high-profile data breaches" after founder Christina Cacioppo discovered that the Product Security lead at Dropbox "spent a ton of time explaining how the product had been made secure to his higher-ups, so they could help in selling the product." That is a scene, not a thesis. The reader can picture the problem.

Mercury's Immad Akhund framed the founding this way: "I had the idea for Mercury in 2013 after noticing a sharp contrast in how quickly startup tooling was evolving — Stripe, Gusto, and Rippling had dramatically improved, but banking remained stubbornly outdated." Again: contrast, specificity, date.

**Move 2: Name the gap with precision, not abstraction.**
Drata's mission is "to be the trust layer between great companies." That is abstract — but they paired it with a concrete value claim: "automated compliance helping startups achieve SOC 2 without the months of manual work." The abstract mission lands because it is grounded by a specific friction point (months of manual work).

Policy Canary's equivalent: "Nobody does product-level FDA monitoring for food/supplement/cosmetics." That sentence should appear in the post, nearly verbatim. It is specific and falsifiable.

**Move 3: Explain why now, with external evidence.**
Good founding posts use market timing as a structural argument: "The regulation just changed" or "The enforcement environment shifted" or "The old tools were built for a world that no longer exists." This removes the "why did you wait?" question and replaces it with "why didn't this exist sooner?"

**Move 4: Build credibility through specificity, not credentials.**
Vanta did not say "we have 20 years of security experience." They said "word of mouth was spreading — two or three emails a week" and "the first SOC 2 certification in Colorado." Traction as proof. The lesson: show the reader that you understand their problem deeply enough to have built something that works, rather than telling them you are trustworthy.

**Move 5: Give the reader a job to do.**
Every good founding post ends with a clear, low-friction next action. Not "request a demo." Something like: "Here is a real example of what you would have received if you were subscribed last week." Show, do not pitch.

### What to avoid

- Credential-leading openings ("We are a team of former FDA regulators and compliance experts...") — these are the equivalent of a LinkedIn summary that opens with "results-oriented professional."
- Generic problem statements that apply to every industry ("Compliance is complex and expensive and time-consuming...").
- Defensive positioning ("Unlike other tools, we...") — this signals insecurity and makes the reader wonder what you are defending against.
- Feature lists in founding posts. Features are for the pricing page. The founding post is about the problem and the belief.

## 2. FDA REGULATORY PAIN POINTS — SPECIFIC AND TIMELY (MARCH 2026)

These are verified, sourced data points for use in the post. They are specific enough to be credible and recent enough to feel urgent.

### The enforcement surge

**Warning letters up 73% (H2 2025).** Between July 1 and December 3, 2025, the FDA issued 327 warning letters — a 73% increase over the same period in 2024. CDER warning letters separately jumped 50% in fiscal year 2025, confirmed by Jill Furman, director of CDER's Office of Compliance, at the 2025 Enforcement, Litigation, and Compliance Conference.

**Untitled letters exploded.** The FDA issued 58 untitled letters in fiscal year 2025 — up from 5 in 2024 and 4 in 2023. These are the formal warnings that precede warning letters.

**Inspections up significantly.** Food and cosmetics industries saw 1,540 more inspections in 2025 than 2024.

**Supplement-specific.** At least 5 social media-related warning letters to supplement companies in Q2 2025 alone. Q3 2025 saw 14 warning letters to ophthalmic supplement/eye drop companies in a single quarter.

**What a warning letter costs.** The project brief documents this: $25,000–$100,000+ for a single warning letter response. Average single-product recall: $10M (FDA estimate).

### The DOGE/FDA capacity collapse

This is the most counterintuitive pain point — fewer regulators means more risk for compliant brands, not less.

- FDA lost **3,859 employees in 2025** plus **473 more in 2026**. Currently ~20% understaffed across the board.
- The Human Foods Program saw "indiscriminate firing of 89 staff" including specialists in nutrition, infant formula, food safety response, and chemical safety review (Deputy Commissioner Jim Jones's words on resignation).
- USDA lost 22,253 jobs. Food Safety Inspection Service lost 913 positions.
- Marion Nestle (Emerita Professor, NYU): "Without them, companies are on their own."
- Don Schaffner (Department Chair, Rutgers): "Fewer inspectors mean fewer inspections and longer intervals between inspections" — the "biggest concern" is institutional knowledge loss.
- FDA foreign inspections dropped approximately 50%.

**The brand owner implication:** When FDA capacity drops, enforcement becomes MORE targeted and less random. The FDA uses data-driven targeting. A company that does not self-monitor is flying blind while the agency is specifically looking for easy targets.

Source: [FDA and USDA staff cuts under Trump raise food safety risks](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/02/16/fda-and-usda-staff-cuts-under-trump-raise-food-safety-risks/)

### MoCRA — the cosmetics compliance pressure

**What it is.** The Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act of 2022 is the biggest expansion of FDA authority over cosmetics since 1938. Before MoCRA, cosmetics were essentially self-regulated. Now:
- Mandatory facility registration and product listing (deadline: July 1, 2024)
- Serious adverse event reporting required
- FDA mandatory recall authority for unsafe cosmetics (guidance released December 18, 2025)
- GMP regulations coming (rulemaking delayed past statutory December 2024 deadline — now "long-term actions")
- PFAS safety evaluation released December 29, 2025: 51 PFAS in 1,744 cosmetic formulations
- Fragrance allergen disclosure rule: delayed past June 2024 statutory deadline, still pending

**Biennial registration renewal: July 2026.** The project brief flags this. 9,500+ registered facilities must re-register. For many small brands, the first registration was done hastily in 2024 under enforcement pressure — the renewal is the first time they will encounter the full ongoing compliance burden.

**The small business exception trap.** MoCRA exempts businesses with <$1M average gross annual US sales from facility registration and product listing — UNLESS products regularly contact mucous membranes of the eye, are injected, are for internal use, or alter appearance for more than 24 hours. Most eye products and many color cosmetics from small brands do not qualify for the exemption.

### The state patchwork — a new monitoring category

This is the pain point that did not exist two years ago and is now arguably worse than federal regulation for multi-state brands.

- **140+ food additive bills across 38 states in 2025** (project brief).
- **1,385 Prop 65 notices in the first 4 months of 2025** (project brief).
- **California AB 496:** 26 additional cosmetic ingredients banned, effective January 1, 2027 — on top of the 24 banned by the Toxic-Free Cosmetics Act effective January 1, 2025.
- **State PFAS bans expanding:** New Hampshire, New Jersey, Oregon, Rhode Island (2027), New Mexico (2028), Illinois (2032) — each with different timelines and ingredient lists.
- **NY supplement age restrictions** (project brief).
- **CBD/hemp cliff:** New federal hemp rules effective November 2026 — thousands of products become illegal.

**The operational implication for brands:** A cosmetics brand selling nationally now needs to track 50 different regulatory frameworks, not one. The compliance burden has multiplied without any increase in legal/regulatory staff at small companies.

Source: [State PFAS Bans in Cosmetics Expand Ahead of 2026](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/12/state-pfas-bans-in-cosmetics-expand-ahead-of-2026)

### The MAHA/Kennedy regulatory chaos

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. at HHS has created a period of regulatory uncertainty that is simultaneously unsettling for brands relying on existing GRAS approvals and creating new enforcement exposure:
- **Red No. 3 ban:** Effective January 2027 — brands must reformulate.
- **All petroleum-based food dyes:** Banned by end of 2027 (project brief).
- **GRAS reform:** Proposed rule requiring companies to submit notices for ALL food substances claimed to be GRAS (including indirect additives through packaging). This affects thousands of ingredients currently in market without formal FDA clearance.
- **Supplement deregulation signals** are confusing — some brands are interpreting this as reduced risk, but enforcement is simultaneously increasing.

## 3. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE — LANGUAGE ANALYSIS FOR DIFFERENTIATION

### How the incumbents position themselves

**AgencyIQ (by Politico)**
- Headline: "Get distilled insights and analysis from experts in life sciences, food, and chemicals regulations — so you can see more, faster."
- Sub-positioning: "Regulation ready expertise" / "Everything you need to understand, plan for, and shape regulation"
- Claims: "7000+ insights," "100+ years combined regulatory experience," "non-partisan newsroom"
- Features: Inspection Navigator, Collections, Legislative Tracker
- Target customer: Enterprise regulatory teams, pharmaceutical/life sciences professionals. Former FDA practitioners writing for regulatory affairs professionals.
- Pricing: No visible pricing. Free newsletter entry point, trial available.
- Voice: Authoritative, expert, newsroom-adjacent.

**The gap AgencyIQ creates:** They are a regulatory journalism and intelligence service for professionals who already understand regulation. They tell you what happened. They do not tell you which of YOUR products it affects.

**RegASK**
- Tagline: "Agentic AI Regulatory Intelligence Platform"
- Coverage: Life Sciences and Consumer Products — food, supplements, cosmetics, personal care
- Positioning: "Empowering global organizations in highly regulated sectors" — proactive monitoring, actionable insights, workflow automation
- Target: Enterprise compliance teams. "Global organizations."
- Key claim: Flexibility "to align with strategy" and "target deep expertise globally."
- Voice: Enterprise/consulting. Uses terms like "workflow orchestration" and "GenAI."

**The gap RegASK creates:** Enterprise-priced, enterprise-targeted, global focus, agentic workflow automation. Not built for a founder at a $2M supplement brand who does not have a compliance team.

**SGS Digicomply**
- Positioning: "All-in-one Food Safety & Regulatory Compliance software"
- Trust signals: Nestlé, Mars, Coca-Cola, PepsiCo as named clients.
- Features: Real-time monitoring, predictive risk management, regulatory compliance, horizon scanning
- Voice: Enterprise safety + quality. Food manufacturer focused.

**The gap SGS Digicomply creates:** Built for food manufacturers with dedicated quality/safety teams. Priced and designed for Nestlé, not a 10-person supplement brand. The Nestlé logos are a trust signal that simultaneously signals "not for you" to a small brand founder.

**FoodChain ID**
- Positioning: Integrated food safety, quality, and sustainability services
- Services: Product certifications, regulatory compliance, testing, consulting
- Voice: Full-service consulting/testing lab, not software.
- Gap: Consulting model, not monitoring SaaS.

### The language gap — what none of them say

None of these competitors use language like:
- "Your products" (personal, possessive)
- "Which of MY products is affected" (buyer's actual question)
- "Your Gold Standard Whey" / "Your Marine Collagen Powder" (specific product names)
- "Weekly all-clear" (peace of mind framing)
- Event-driven, product-specific alerts

They all speak to regulatory categories and compliance processes. None speak to products.

**The key linguistic differentiation for Policy Canary:**
- Competitors say: "Monitor the regulatory landscape"
- Policy Canary says: "Monitor YOUR products"
- Competitors say: "Regulatory intelligence"
- Policy Canary says: "An alert about your specific products"
- Competitors say: "Compliance workflows"
- Policy Canary says: "You wake up to an email that says: your Marine Collagen Powder is affected. Here is why. Here is what to do."

## 4. TRUST SIGNALS THAT RESONATE WITH SMALL BRAND OWNERS

Based on the research, small supplement and food brand owners are not primarily looking for credentials — they are looking for evidence that the tool understands their actual problem.

### What builds trust

**1. Show the actual output, not just the feature.**
The highest-impact trust signal for Policy Canary is a real sample Product Intelligence Email addressed to a real product. Not a screenshot of a dashboard. Not a feature list. The email itself — personalized, specific, actionable. "This is what you would have received last Tuesday if you were subscribed and had Gold Standard Whey in your account."

This mirrors the Vanta pattern: they showed the actual spreadsheet before they showed the product. Traction before technology.

**2. Show the source.**
Small brand owners are appropriately skeptical of AI-generated regulatory analysis. "Where does this come from?" is the first trust question. The trust answer is: "Every alert links to the source document — the Federal Register notice, the warning letter, the FDA guidance. We do not make claims without citations."

Source transparency is a differentiator because the enterprise tools (AgencyIQ's newsroom, RegASK's expert team) do not make their methodology visible. Policy Canary can win trust precisely by being more transparent about data sources, not less.

**3. The "one warning letter" framing.**
The research confirms: one FDA warning letter costs $25,000–$100,000+ to respond to. One product recall averages $10M. Small supplement firms with fewer than 20 employees spend $46,000/year on GMP compliance. Policy Canary at $99/month is $1,188/year — less than one hour of regulatory consultant time at $150–$500/hour.

This is not a features-and-benefits frame. It is a risk/insurance frame. Small brand owners understand risk pricing. They buy insurance for their inventory, their facility, their employees. This is insurance for their regulatory exposure.

**4. The "we know your products" demonstration.**
The single most effective trust-building move is demonstrating, in the post itself, that the system knows how to do the mapping. Write out the example:
> "The FDA proposed tightening identity testing requirements for whey protein (21 CFR 111). If you had Gold Standard Whey in your account, here is what you would have received within 24 hours: [example email structure]. If you do not have a whey protein product, you would not have received that alert. Because it is not relevant to your products."

This shows the intelligence of the system without requiring the reader to understand how it works.

**5. Specificity of pain.**
The research confirms that indie/small brand owners feel the compliance burden acutely in a specific way: the documentation and mapping burden. "Many indie brands have relied on supplier spec sheets or anecdotal testing rather than comprehensive safety substantiation now required." The pain is not "regulation is complex." The pain is: "I got a Federal Register alert about BHA. I have 47 products. Which ones contain BHA? At what levels? How much time do I have? What do I need to do?"

That specific pain — the mapping from "what happened" to "which of my products" — is the post's entire argument. Name it explicitly.

**6. Ongoing monitoring, not one-time check.**
The trust gap in the market is temporal: existing tools check compliance today. Nobody monitors for compliance tomorrow. Frame this as the distinction: "Your formula might be compliant today. The question is whether it will still be compliant in 90 days. The FDA proposed that rule on a Tuesday. Did you hear about it before your next trade show?"

## 5. POST STRUCTURE RECOMMENDATION

Based on the research, here is the recommended architecture for the founding post:

**Title options:**
- "The FDA Changed Something Last Tuesday. Do You Know If It Affects Your Products?"
- "Why Nobody Monitors the FDA for YOUR Specific Products (and What We Built Instead)"
- "We Built Policy Canary Because Warning Letters Are Preventable"

**Opening:** Do not introduce the company. Open with a scene — a specific regulatory change, a specific date, a specific product. "On [date], the FDA issued a warning letter to [company type] for [violation]. The company had [X] products. Only one contained the cited ingredient. They had no system to know which one."

**Section 1 — The specific, timely problem.**
- FDA warning letters up 73% in H2 2025
- FDA down 3,859 employees — "self-monitoring is no longer optional, it's the only option"
- MoCRA: 9,500+ registered facilities, biennial renewal July 2026
- State patchwork: 140+ bills across 38 states
- The real pain: the mapping step. BHA in 47 products. Which ones? Which levels? What timeline?

**Section 2 — What exists today (and why it fails small brands).**
- AgencyIQ: expert regulatory journalism for enterprise teams. Tells you what happened. Does not tell you which of YOUR products is affected.
- RegASK: $25K+ enterprise platform for global compliance operations.
- Free Federal Register alerts: tells you the rule changed. Does not do the mapping.
- Consultants: $150–$500/hour, reactive, not scalable.
- The gap: nobody does product-level monitoring for brands under $50M revenue.

**Section 3 — The belief (not the features).**
- "We believe the question 'which of my products is affected?' should be answered automatically, not manually."
- "We believe a $2M supplement brand deserves the same regulatory early warning as a $200M one."
- "We believe that 'all clear — nothing affecting your products this week' is as valuable as an alert. Peace of mind is part of the value."

**Section 4 — How it works (one paragraph, no jargon).**
You tell us your products. We watch the FDA for every change — proposed rules, final rules, warning letters, guidance documents, recalls, safety alerts. When something changes that could affect one of your specific products, we tell you immediately: which product, why it matters, what the deadline is, what to do next. If nothing changes, we tell you that too — "all clear."

**Section 5 — The proof (show the output).**
Show a real or near-real example Product Intelligence Email. Or describe it in enough detail that the reader can picture it: "Your Marine Collagen Powder. FDA warning letter — identity testing failures for marine-sourced collagen. Three companies cited in the last 12 months. Testing protocol update needed by Q3. Here are three action items."

**Closing — The invitation.**
Not "sign up." The invitation: sign up for the free weekly FDA update (the content marketing layer). "Get what happened at FDA this week. No products required. If you like it, you can add your products and get alerts specific to them."

## 7. WHAT TO AVOID IN THE POST

- Do not claim "AI-powered" as a headline feature. Every tool in this market now claims AI. It is table stakes, not differentiation. Lead with the outcome (product-level alerts), not the technology.
- Do not use the phrase "regulatory intelligence." That is the enterprise tools' category. Policy Canary is in "product monitoring" — a different mental model.
- Do not lead with credentials or team bios. The reader does not care yet. Earn attention first.
- Do not reference "compliance" as the value. The value is early warning and peace of mind. Compliance is the output, not the experience.
- Do not promise certainty. The post should include one honest caveat: "We show you the source for every alert. We do the mapping, you make the decision. We are your early warning system, not your legal counsel."
